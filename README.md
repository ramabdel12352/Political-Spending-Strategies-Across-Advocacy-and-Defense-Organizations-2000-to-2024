# Political-Spending-Strategies-Across-Advocacy-and-Defense-Organizations-2000-to-2024
This project analyzes how major advocacy organizations and defense corporations in the United States allocate political spending between lobbying and campaign contributions over a twenty four year period from 2000 to 2024. Using large scale data from OpenSecrets, the study examines more than forty organizations to understand whether political actors treat these two forms of spending as complementary strategies where both increase together or as substitutes where resources shift depending on political conditions.

The core goal of this research is to explain how different types of organizations build and maintain political influence. Defense corporations and advocacy groups operate in fundamentally different policy environments and this project shows how those differences shape their long term strategies. Defense firms which operate in stable and predictable policy systems such as military spending tend to rely on consistent and reinforcing investments in both lobbying and campaign contributions. In contrast advocacy organizations working in more volatile issue areas like civil rights environmental policy and labor adjust their spending based on election cycles public attention and shifts in political power.

The project is grounded in the Advocacy Coalition Framework which helps explain how organizations behave within stable versus unstable policy subsystems. By applying this framework the analysis demonstrates that organizations in stable environments prioritize long term access and consistency while those in more uncertain environments prioritize flexibility and timing.

Key findings from the analysis include:

Defense corporations consistently use complementary strategies increasing lobbying and campaign contributions together to maintain long term access to policymakers
Advocacy organizations rely on substitution or mixed strategies shifting spending between lobbying and elections depending on political opportunities
Partisan alignment plays a major role with Republican leaning organizations more likely to exhibit stable complementary behavior while Democratic leaning organizations show more flexible and cyclical patterns
Election cycles significantly impact advocacy groups leading to spikes in campaign contributions during high stakes elections
Organizational structure and policy stability are critical drivers of political strategy not just financial resources

The project combines descriptive trend analysis statistical modeling and data visualization to provide a comprehensive view of political spending behavior. Visualizations such as time series graphs and scatterplots highlight how spending patterns diverge between organization types and how partisan alignment influences strategic decisions. For example defense corporations maintain steady lobbying with gradual contribution increases while advocacy groups display sharp electoral spikes and shifting priorities.

Beyond identifying patterns this research contributes to broader debates about money in politics. It challenges the assumption that all interest groups use political spending in the same way instead showing that strategy is shaped by institutional context access to power and long term organizational goals. The findings suggest that political influence is not simply about spending more money but about how and when that money is deployed.

This repository is designed for students researchers and anyone interested in American politics interest groups campaign finance and lobbying. It provides both empirical evidence and theoretical insight into how political organizations operate over time offering a deeper understanding of the relationship between money strategy and influence in the US political system.
